Game Development Field Guide

If you are new to game development, spend some time here: build the right mindset, understand why the work is worth doing, and benefit from lessons the community has already learned.

TL;DR

This is a working field guide for the maker of Manipuri Life, future contributors, and anyone learning game development. Its purpose is to develop sound judgment across the many parts of making a game, keep time, effort, and resources tied to a clear reason, and avoid repeating preventable mistakes by learning from what experienced developers and the wider game development community have already shared.

Why this guide exists

Game development involves thousands of decisions across design, art, audio, engineering, production, testing, marketing, and collaboration. Knowing a technique is useful; knowing how to think about the decision, why it matters, and whether it deserves investment is more durable. This guide collects ideas that improve that judgment.

Develop the right mindset
Learn useful ways to reason about players, quality, iteration, scope, trade-offs, uncertainty, and the different disciplines involved in making a game. The goal is not a list of rigid rules, but better questions and clearer judgment in unfamiliar situations.
Be clear about the investment
Before spending time, effort, money, or attention, understand what outcome the work is meant to create, what uncertainty it reduces, and how it contributes to the player's experience or the project's ability to ship. Activity by itself is not progress.
Learn before repeating mistakes
Game developers, researchers, critics, and communities have already shared decades of successes, failures, patterns, and practical advice. Use that accumulated experience as a starting advantage: study what others learned, understand the context, and avoid paying again for lessons already available.

Treat it as a multi-year commitment

Even a small commercial game commonly takes years, not months, to design, build, revise, test, finish, release, and support. A useful early planning assumption is one to five years, depending on scope, experience, team size, working hours, tools, funding, and the quality bar. It is not a rule: a deliberately tiny game may be faster, while ambitious art, deep systems, platform support, or repeated redesign can push development far beyond five years.

This is not a fun little excursion

Making a serious game cannot rest on a passing fancy. The early excitement of a new idea is brief; the work that follows includes years of repetition, uncertainty, discarded work, debugging, testing, prioritization, and finishing details that are rarely glamorous. Get the motivation right before making the commitment. Curiosity is enough reason to build a weekend prototype; it is not, by itself, a plan for sustaining a production.

Ask before you commit

Why must this game exist?
State the personal, creative, cultural, commercial, or technical reason clearly. A durable motivation must survive after novelty fades and other people are not yet paying attention.
What does success mean?
Decide whether success means learning, finishing, reaching a particular audience, earning revenue, advancing a craft, or creating a specific player experience. Different answers justify very different investments.
What am I genuinely willing to invest?
Estimate sustainable hours per week, a realistic number of years, money at risk, skills to learn, and responsibilities that cannot be sacrificed. A plan that requires permanent overwork is not a plan.
Will the motivation survive the ordinary work?
Imagine the hundredth playtest, a difficult rewrite, months without visible progress, and the final accessibility, localization, platform, store, and support tasks. Be willing to do the work of making the game, not only enjoy the identity of being someone who is making one.

Commit in stages, not in fantasy

Taking the commitment seriously does not mean promising five years on day one. First commit to research, then to a focused proof of concept, then to a playable core, and only then to production. At each stage, use evidence to decide whether the game deserves the next investment. A clear decision to stop, reduce scope, or begin again can be a successful outcome.

Ideas worth carrying

Short statements can make a principle easier to remember, but brevity should not flatten the idea. Each entry records both the line and the practical lesson we want to carry into development.

account_tree

“A game is a series of interesting decisions.”

Commonly attributed to Sid Meier. The useful test is not merely whether the player can choose, but whether the options are understandable, meaningfully different, and capable of changing what happens next.

reviews

Players judge the result, not the effort

No one experiences how long a feature took to make; they experience whether it is good. Effort can explain cost and guide production decisions, but it cannot substitute for quality in the player's hands.

Appeal gets the first chance; engagement earns the next

A good game needs both. They answer different questions at different points in the player's journey, so success in one cannot be used as evidence that the other is working.

Appeal · Will someone give it a chance?
Appeal is the top of the funnel: will someone notice the game, look closer, download it, or try it? It may come from the premise, visual identity, setting, a distinctive mechanic, a compelling moment in a trailer, or the way other players describe it.
Engagement · Will someone want to continue?
Engagement begins once play starts: are the actions, decisions, feedback, goals, pacing, and discoveries strong enough to hold attention? The promise that created appeal now has to be fulfilled through play.

Do not confuse the two

A marketable hook can earn a first session and still lead to a dull game. A deeply engaging system can remain undiscovered if nothing makes people curious enough to try it. Diagnose the stage where interest is being lost before deciding what to improve.

The whole is made from moments

The overall game must be good, but every element that makes it up should also be enjoyable and convincing in its own right. A strong genre loop cannot excuse interactions that feel weak every time the player touches them.

The sword-swing test

Imagine a survival-crafting game with a particular sword. Set the crafting tree and survival strategy aside for a moment. When the player swings that sword, does the timing feel responsive? Does the motion communicate weight? Does the sound make contact satisfying? The sword is part of a larger system, but swinging it is also a repeated experience of its own. It has to feel good at that scale.

Apply the same test to walking, selecting an item, opening a menu, placing an object, harvesting a crop, starting a conversation, and hearing a reward. Not every action needs spectacle, but every repeated action should support the feeling the game intends to create.

Make proofs of concept

A proof of concept exists to answer a specific uncertain question cheaply: can the movement feel right, can this art pipeline work, can the world stream reliably, or is the core activity enjoyable? Build the smallest experiment that can produce useful evidence.

  1. Name the question

    Write down what uncertainty the POC is meant to reduce and what result would count as useful evidence.

  2. Build only enough to learn

    Do not quietly turn the experiment into a miniature production codebase. Optimize for speed of learning, not completeness or permanence.

  3. Keep the right to throw it away

    A POC is disposable by design. Preserve the lesson, not necessarily the implementation. Starting the production version again may be the correct outcome.

Be careful with “just good enough”

Shipping requires compromise, and not every detail deserves unlimited time. The danger is passive acceptance: feeling that something is wrong, knowing why it matters, but ceasing to push because fixing it will be difficult.

Conscious trade-off

State the shortcoming, understand its player impact, compare it with higher priorities, record the decision, and choose deliberately whether to fix, defer, redesign, or cut it.

Passive acceptance

Call it good enough because pushback is tiring, leave the concern vague, and let the next system build on top of a weak foundation. Repeated compromises of this kind compound until the whole is no longer worth what it cost to make.

“Good enough” is healthy when it is a deliberate quality bar matched to the importance of the work. It is harmful when it is a euphemism for giving up on a problem that still threatens the experience.
