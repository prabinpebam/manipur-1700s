Do Not Ignore Marketing

Marketing gives a good game a fair chance to be discovered, understood, tried, and shared. It is part of making the game viable, not an expensive campaign added at the end.

TL;DR

Marketing creates opportunity; the game must convert and retain it. For an indie team, marketing usually means understanding the audience, expressing the hook clearly, building a useful store page and demo, earning wishlists, reaching suitable creators and press, participating in relevant events, and cultivating word of mouth over time. It does not require a large advertising budget. It does require deliberate work throughout development, because a game nobody encounters cannot be judged on its quality.

Research foundation Strategy comes next

This page establishes the mindset and an initial evidence base. It will grow into a concrete marketing strategy for Manipuri Life: audience segments, positioning, comparable games, channel experiments, creator fit, store-page testing, demo and festival plans, wishlist targets, launch sequencing, budget, measurement, and post-launch communication.

The principle

Do not treat marketing as an apology for a weak game, and do not treat quality as permission to ignore discovery. Both failures come from asking one system to do the other's job.

Marketing earns the chance

It helps the right people notice the game, understand why it may be for them, and take the next step: watch, wishlist, download a demo, buy, play, or tell someone else.

The game earns what follows

Appeal, onboarding, game feel, depth, reliability, and sustained enjoyment determine whether attention becomes play, recommendation, community, and durable success.

Marketing is not everything; ignoring it is still a mistake

Excellent marketing may produce a spike for a disappointing game, but it cannot manufacture lasting affection. An excellent game with no clear route to an audience may never receive enough trials to prove itself. The goal is not maximum attention. It is qualified attention from people likely to value the experience, arriving when the game is ready to make good on its promise.

Think of marketing as a system

Marketing is the path between a game and the people for whom it was made. That path begins before launch and contains several distinct jobs. Diagnose which job is failing instead of saying only “we need more marketing.”

  1. Audience · Who is this specifically for?

    Know the players, communities, adjacent games, unmet desires, and language they already use. “Everyone” is not an actionable audience.

  2. Positioning · Why this game?

    Express the recognizable category and the meaningful difference. A player should be able to understand the promise without first reading the design document.

  3. Awareness · How will the right people encounter it?

    Creators, festivals, platform discovery, press, communities, search, social posts, collaborations, and word of mouth are distribution paths. Choose them for audience fit, not fashion.

  4. Intent · Can interest become a future action?

    A clear store page, strong capsule and trailer, wishlist, mailing list, community, or demo turns a brief encounter into a measurable signal and a route back.

  5. Conversion · Does the promise survive contact?

    The price, page, reviews, demo, onboarding, performance, and opening play experience determine whether interested people begin and continue.

  6. Advocacy · Is the game worth sharing?

    Memorable moments, player stories, creator-friendly expression, updates, community care, and the game's underlying quality give players reasons to carry it to the next person.

Case study · Among Us and the opportunity-quality loop

Among Us released in 2018 and had already reached one million mobile downloads by August 2019, but it had not become a global phenomenon. In January 2020, its three-person developer Innersloth described the game as complete and wrote that after a few fixes it would “let Among Us take its natural course.”

The developer's position before the breakout

“Among Us has had a very good run.” After outlining final changes, Innersloth said: “then we will let Among Us take its natural course.” The team was preparing to move on, months before the explosion in creator attention. Innersloth, January 2020

PC Gamer's reconstruction of the breakout shows what happened next. At the start of July 2020, the game averaged only a few hundred Twitch viewers; by the end of August it averaged more than 100,000. Early activity from creators such as SR_Kaif, AdmiralBulldog, and singsing was followed by major Korean and English-language streamer groups, including Sodapoppin and xQc. The streams produced discovery at enormous scale, and player counts followed.

But “Twitch made it popular” is only half the explanation. The game was unusually able to use that opportunity:

It generated human stories
Accusations, deception, mistakes, betrayals, and reactions made each match entertaining to watch and easy to retell. The creators were not merely showing mechanics; they were producing social episodes.
It multiplied creators
A lobby brought several streamers together, combining communities and giving each participant a reason to expose the game to their own audience.
It had a low barrier to play
Free mobile versions, a low PC price, modest hardware requirements, simple controls, and cross-platform access made it easy for viewers to become players.
It crossed languages and communities
Korean, Portuguese, Spanish, and English support helped adoption move through distinct creator communities rather than depending on one English-language spike.
The underlying game held up
The social-deduction core was approachable, replayable, and capable of producing new situations. The exposure opened the door; the game gave groups a reason to keep returning.

The lesson is not “wait for Twitch”

The breakout was not a repeatable campaign plan. The useful lesson is to understand how the game travels: whether it is legible to a viewer, creates moments worth clipping or discussing, fits particular creators, and makes the jump from watching to playing easy. Then conduct targeted outreach and experiments rather than hoping an algorithm discovers it by accident.

Case study · Hades made community part of development

Supergiant did not use Early Access merely as a storefront label. The studio designed Hades around an ongoing relationship with players, releasing into Early Access in December 2018 and reaching version 1.0 in September 2020.

A first-party statement of intent

“Our foremost goal was to see if we could create something great in partnership with our community.” Supergiant adds that the game's modular structure and narrative approach flowed from that decision. Supergiant Games, Hades FAQ

The lesson is not that every game should use Early Access. It is that product development, communication, feedback, and audience building can reinforce one another. Regular meaningful updates gave existing players reasons to return and talk, gave new players evidence that the game was improving, and let the studio learn before the full launch. Marketing here was not separate hype; it was a credible public development rhythm supported by a game designed to evolve with feedback.

Case study · Stardew Valley became larger than its creator

Stardew Valley demonstrates the compounding stage after discovery: a good game becomes a platform for player expression. Ten years after launch, Eric Barone described a community producing “fan art, memes, videos, mods, theories, guides, and more,” and reflected that the world had become “this massive thing that is largely external to me.”

This is not a launch tactic that can be scheduled. It is advocacy earned over time. Continued updates, creator coverage, guides, mods, fan work, and recommendations each create another route by which someone can encounter the game. Marketing begins as work by the developer; at its strongest, it becomes expression by people who genuinely care about what the game gave them.

The product remains the foundation

Barone closes the same reflection with a useful priority: “At the end of the day, games are supposed to be FUN. That will always be the priority in my work.” ConcernedApe, Stardew Valley 10-year anniversary

This does not require big-budget advertising

Money can buy reach, production, expertise, and repeated experiments. It cannot remove the need for a clear audience and a compelling game. A small team can begin with time-intensive, focused work that creates reusable assets and direct learning.

Low budget does not mean no cost

Research, editing trailers, preparing demos, writing updates, answering creators, maintaining a community, running tests, and measuring results all consume scarce development time. Budget that work explicitly. “Organic” describes how attention spreads; it does not mean nobody did the work that made spreading possible.

Build it into production

Do not finish the game and then ask who might want it. At each stage, marketing should answer a question that can still change a decision.

StageMarketing questionUseful evidence
ConceptIs there a reachable audience for this promise?Comparable games, communities, player language, demand patterns

Guardrails

Do not confuse virality with strategy
Study why content travels, but never build a forecast that requires an unpredictable breakout.
Do not chase an audience the game does not serve
Large but poorly matched reach creates weak conversion, disappointment, and misleading feedback.
Do not let metrics replace judgment
Wishlists, views, click-through rates, and followers are signals. Pair them with play behavior, interviews, reviews, and an understanding of where the traffic came from.
Do not manufacture false urgency or intimacy
Communicate honestly. Avoid manipulative scarcity, fake grassroots enthusiasm, undisclosed sponsorships, spam, and community performance that the team cannot sustain.
Do not wait for launch
Launch concentrates work already done. It is a poor time to discover that the positioning is unclear, the store page does not convert, creators cannot use the game, or nobody knows it exists.
